A probability measure P on ($\Omega, \mathcal{F}$) must satisfy:
Definitions: A probability measure $P$ on $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$ is a function $P: \mathcal{F} \to [0,1]$ satisfying: (1) $P(\Omega) = 1$ (normalization), (2) $P(\emptyset) = 0$, and (3) $\sigma$-additivity: for disjoint sets $A_1, A_2, \ldots \in \mathcal{F}$, we have $P(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i) = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} P(A_i)$. Reasoning: Option B correctly states both normalization ($P(\Omega) = 1$) and $\sigma$-additivity (countable additivity). Option A is wrong ($P(\Omega) = 1$, not 0). Option C is wrong (events can have zero probability). Option D is insufficient ($\sigma$-additivity is stronger than finite additivity). Answer: A probability measure must satisfy $P(\Omega) = 1$ and be $\sigma$-additive on disjoint sets.
