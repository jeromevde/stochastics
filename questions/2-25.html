For 2D Brownian motion ($B_1$, $B_2$) with correlation $\rho$, the quadratic covariation [$B_1$, $B_2$]$_t$ equals:
Definitions: For two stochastic processes $X$ and $Y$, the quadratic covariation $[X, Y]_t$ is defined as: $$[X, Y]_t = \lim_{\|\Pi\| \to 0} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (X(t_i) - X(t_{i-1}))(Y(t_i) - Y(t_{i-1}))$$ where $\Pi = \{0 = t_0 < t_1 < \cdots < t_n = t\}$ is a partition of $[0,t]$ and $\|\Pi\| = \max_i(t_i - t_{i-1})$ is the mesh size. For correlated Brownian motions $(B_1, B_2)$ with correlation $\rho$, we have $\text{Cov}(B_1(t), B_2(t)) = \rho t$. Reasoning: Consider a partition $\Pi$ of $[0,t]$. The increments $\Delta B_1^i = B_1(t_i) - B_1(t_{i-1})$ and $\Delta B_2^i = B_2(t_i) - B_2(t_{i-1})$ are jointly normal with: $$E[\Delta B_1^i \cdot \Delta B_2^i] = \text{Cov}(\Delta B_1^i, \Delta B_2^i) = \rho(t_i - t_{i-1})$$ Summing over the partition and taking the limit as $\|\Pi\| \to 0$: $$[B_1, B_2]_t = \lim_{\|\Pi\| \to 0} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \Delta B_1^i \cdot \Delta B_2^i = \lim_{\|\Pi\| \to 0} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \rho(t_i - t_{i-1}) = \rho t$$ This is often written informally as $dB_1 \cdot dB_2 = \rho \, dt$. Answer: The quadratic covariation is $[B_1, B_2]_t = \rho t$. Practical Use Case: In multi-asset derivatives pricing (e.g., basket options, spread options), the correlation $\rho$ between asset returns directly affects option values through this covariation. For example, in pricing a spread option on two correlated stocks, the payoff depends on $(S_1 - S_2)^+$, and the variance of the spread involves $[B_1, B_2]_t = \rho t$, which determines how the two assets move together.
