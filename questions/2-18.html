The Itô integral $\int_0^t$ f(s,B(s)) dB(s) evaluates the integrand at:
Definitions: The Itô integral $\int_0^t f(s, B(s)) \, dB(s)$ is defined as the limit: $$\int_0^t f(s, B(s)) \, dB(s) = \lim_{\|\Pi\| \to 0} \sum_{i} f(t_{i-1}, B(t_{i-1}))(B(t_i) - B(t_{i-1}))$$ The choice of evaluation point affects the integral's value and properties. Reasoning: The Itô integral uses left-endpoint evaluation: $\sum f(t_{i-1}, B(t_{i-1}))(B(t_i) - B(t_{i-1}))$. This ensures the integrand at each step is independent of the subsequent Brownian increment, which is crucial for the integral to be a martingale. Other evaluation points (midpoint, right endpoint) lead to different integrals with different properties. Answer: The Itô integral evaluates the integrand at the left endpoint of each partition interval. Practical Use Case: Left-endpoint evaluation ensures that hedging strategies are implementable in practice. When computing $\int_0^T \Delta_t dS_t$ (cumulative gains from delta hedging), we use $\Delta_t$ based on time-$t$ information, not future information. This makes $\int_0^T \Delta_t dB_t$ a martingale under $\mathbb{Q}$, which is essential for the replication argument underlying Black-Scholes. If we used right-endpoint evaluation (Stratonovich integral), we'd have $dB \cdot dB = dt$ instead of Itô's $(dB)^2 = dt$, changing option pricing formulas. The left-endpoint convention is why the drift term appears in Itô's lemma with the factor $\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2}$.
