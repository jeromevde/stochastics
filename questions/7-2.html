The first jump time $\tau$ of a Poisson process $N_\lambda(t)$ has distribution:
Definitions: The first jump time $\tau$ (or first arrival time) of a Poisson process is the random time at which the first event occurs. Inter-arrival times between successive events are i.i.d. and memoryless. Reasoning: The first jump occurs after time $t$ if and only if no events occur in $[0,t]$: $P(\tau > t) = P(N_\lambda(t) = 0) = e^{-\lambda t}$. This is the survival function of an exponential distribution with rate $\lambda$. Therefore $\tau \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$, with density $f(t) = \lambda e^{-\lambda t}$ for $t \geq 0$ and mean $1/\lambda$. This memoryless property is unique to the exponential distribution. Answer: Exponential distribution with rate $\lambda$ (Exp($\lambda$)). Practical Use Case: Credit risk modelers use Poisson processes to model default arrivals in portfolios. The exponential distribution of the first jump time means default times have the memoryless property: the conditional probability of default in the next year is constant, regardless of how long the firm has survived. This simplifies credit derivative pricing (e.g., CDS contracts) where default intensity $\lambda$ can be calibrated from market spreads, and the time-to-default distribution follows immediately from the Poisson framework.
