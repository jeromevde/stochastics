A caplet pays off ($T_2$−$T_1$)$\cdot$max(0, L($T_1$,$T_2$) − K) at time:
Definitions: A caplet is an option on an interest rate. It pays $(T_2-T_1) \cdot \max(0, L(T_1,T_2) - K)$ where $L(T_1,T_2)$ is the LIBOR rate fixed at time $T_1$ for the period $[T_1,T_2]$, and $K$ is the strike rate.

Reasoning: The LIBOR rate $L(T_1,T_2)$ is set (fixed) at the beginning of the accrual period $T_1$, but the payment based on this rate occurs at the end of the period $T_2$. This mimics how floating-rate loans work in practice: the rate is determined at the start, but interest is paid at the end. The payoff multiplies the rate difference by the period length $(T_2-T_1)$ and is paid at $T_2$.

Answer: The caplet pays off at time $T_2$.
