Under a risk-neutral measure Q with bank account numeraire, the value V(t) of a derivative satisfies:
Definitions: The bank account (numeraire) $N(t) = e^{rt}$ grows at the risk-free rate $r$. Under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$, discounted asset prices are martingales. The value $V(t)$ of a derivative is the price at which it can be traded at time $t$. Reasoning: The fundamental pricing formula states that the discounted derivative price forms a martingale: $$\frac{V(t)}{N(t)} = E^\mathbb{Q}\left[\frac{V(T)}{N(T)} \Big| \mathcal{F}_t\right]$$ This means the current discounted price equals the expected discounted payoff under $\mathbb{Q}$. Equivalently: $$V(t) = N(t) E^\mathbb{Q}\left[\frac{V(T)}{N(T)} \Big| \mathcal{F}_t\right] = e^{-r(T-t)} E^\mathbb{Q}[V(T) | \mathcal{F}_t]$$ Answer: The derivative price satisfies $V(t)/N(t) = E^\mathbb{Q}[V(T)/N(T) | \mathcal{F}_t]$. Practical Use Case: This is the workhorse formula for all derivatives pricing. When pricing a European call option, traders compute $C_0 = e^{-rT} E^{\mathbb{Q}}[(S_T - K)^+]$, where the expectation is under the risk-neutral measure. For path-dependent options like Asians, the formula becomes $V_t = e^{-r(T-t)} E^{\mathbb{Q}}[f(S_{t:T}) | \mathcal{F}_t]$ where $f$ depends on the entire future path. In practice, traders implement this via Monte Carlo: simulate many paths under $\mathbb{Q}$, compute payoffs, average, and discount.
