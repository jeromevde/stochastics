The Heston model struggles to fit market prices for:
Definitions: The Heston model uses continuous stochastic volatility to generate the implied volatility smile. However, empirical volatility smiles have different shapes at different maturities, with short-maturity smiles being particularly steep.

Reasoning: Short-maturity options (days to weeks) exhibit steep smiles that are best explained by jump risk - discrete, sudden price movements. The Heston model, with only continuous diffusion dynamics, cannot generate the steep wings observed in short-dated options. For longer maturities, stochastic volatility alone adequately explains the smile shape. This limitation motivates jump-diffusion extensions like Bates or Merton models that combine jumps with stochastic volatility.

Answer: Short-maturity options (because it lacks jumps).
