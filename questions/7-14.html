For pricing vanilla calls via characteristic functions, why multiply C(k) by e^{$\alpha$k}?
Definitions: The Carr-Madan method prices European options using the characteristic function of the log-stock price. The key technical issue is that the option price function $C(k)$ (where $k = \ln(K)$ is log-strike) is not square-integrable, which is required for Fourier transforms. Reasoning: As $k \to -\infty$ (deep in-the-money), $C(k) \to S_0$, so $\int |C(k)|^2 dk = \infty$ (not in $L^2$). To enable Fourier inversion, we multiply by a damping factor $e^{\alpha k}$ with $\alpha > 0$. The modified function $c(k) = e^{\alpha k} C(k)$ decays as $k \to -\infty$ and becomes square-integrable: $\int |c(k)|^2 dk < \infty$. This allows us to compute the Fourier transform and then invert it to recover option prices efficiently. Answer: To make the modified call price square-integrable for Fourier transform. Practical Use Case: This concept is essential for modern financial engineering and quantitative trading. Practitioners apply these principles in derivatives pricing, risk management, and portfolio optimization across asset classes including equities, fixed income, commodities, and FX markets. Understanding this material enables quants to build robust pricing models, traders to identify arbitrage opportunities, and risk managers to compute accurate hedging strategies for complex derivative portfolios.
