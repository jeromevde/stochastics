Definitions: The reflection principle is a key property of Brownian motion relating the maximum process $M_t = \sup_{0 \leq s \leq t} B(s)$ to the terminal value $B(t)$. It exploits the symmetry of Brownian motion. Reasoning: If the maximum exceeds $b$, the process must have crossed level $b$ at some first passage time $\tau$. By the strong Markov property and symmetry, the path after $\tau$ is equally likely to go up or down. The event $\{M_t \geq b\}$ includes all paths with $B(t) \geq b$ plus those that crossed $b$ and came back down. By symmetry: $$P(M_t \geq b) = P(B(t) \geq b) + P(\text{crossed } b \text{ and returned}) = 2P(B(t) \geq b)$$ Answer: The reflection principle gives $P(\sup_{0 \leq s \leq t} B(s) \geq b) = 2P(B(t) \geq b)$ for $b \geq 0$. Practical Use Case: The reflection principle is essential for pricing barrier options and lookback options. For an up-and-out call with barrier $H > K$, we need to compute the probability that the stock price never exceeds $H$ before maturity. Using the reflection principle, we can derive closed-form formulas for barrier option prices without Monte Carlo simulation. Similarly, for lookback options with payoff depending on $\max_{0 \le t \le T} S_t$, the reflection principle provides the distribution of the running maximum, enabling analytic pricing. Investment banks use these formulas to price structured products like reverse convertibles with knock-out features.
