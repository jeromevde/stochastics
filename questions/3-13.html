Definitions: The risk-neutral (equivalent martingale) measure is a probability measure under which all discounted asset prices are martingales. The bank account numeraire $N(t) = e^{rt}$ grows at the risk-free rate $r$. Reasoning: Under the risk-neutral measure, every tradable asset (including derivatives) has expected return equal to the risk-free rate. This is because the discounted price $V(t)/N(t)$ is a martingale, so $E^\mathbb{Q}[V(t+dt)/N(t+dt) | \mathcal{F}_t] = V(t)/N(t)$, which implies $E^\mathbb{Q}[dV/V] = r \, dt$. This property is what makes the measure "risk-neutral" - all risk premiums are removed. Answer: Under the risk-neutral measure, every derivative has expected return equal to the risk-free rate.
