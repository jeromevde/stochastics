The Lévy-Khintchine formula characterizes a Lévy process through its triplet:
Definitions: The Lévy-Khintchine representation theorem states that every Lévy process can be uniquely characterized by a triplet $(a, \sigma, \Pi)$: drift $a \in \mathbb{R}$, Gaussian component volatility $\sigma \geq 0$, and Lévy measure $\Pi$ (a measure on $\mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\}$ describing jump intensities).

Reasoning: The triplet decomposes a Lévy process into three components: (1) deterministic drift $a$, (2) Brownian motion with variance $\sigma^2$, and (3) pure jump part governed by $\Pi$. The Lévy measure must satisfy $\int_{\mathbb{R}} \min(1, x^2) \, \Pi(dx) < \infty$ to ensure finite variation near zero and finite second moment for large jumps. This characterization is complete - the triplet uniquely determines the process.

Answer: $(a, \sigma, \Pi)$ - drift, diffusion, jump intensity measure.
