Two measures P and Q on ($\Omega, \mathcal{F}$) are equivalent if:
Definitions: Two probability measures $P$ and $Q$ on a measurable space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$ are equivalent (written $P \sim Q$) if they have the same null sets: $P(A) = 0 \iff Q(A) = 0$ for all $A \in \mathcal{F}$. Reasoning: Equivalence means that $P$ and $Q$ agree on which events are possible and which are impossible, though they may assign different positive probabilities to possible events. This is crucial in finance: the risk-neutral measure must be equivalent to the real-world measure so that scenarios considered impossible in the real world remain impossible under risk-neutral pricing, ensuring consistency. Answer: Two measures are equivalent if events with probability 0 under one also have probability 0 under the other (and vice versa).
