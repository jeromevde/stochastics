Volatility clustering in the Heston model means:
Definitions: Volatility clustering is an empirical feature of financial returns where periods of high volatility tend to be followed by high volatility, and periods of low volatility by low volatility. It's a form of autocorrelation in volatility (not in returns themselves). Reasoning: The Heston model captures this through its stochastic, mean-reverting variance process. Since $v(t)$ evolves continuously with mean reversion, if $v(t)$ is high today, it's likely to remain relatively high tomorrow (though decaying toward $\theta$). This creates positive autocorrelation in the variance process and in the squared returns, matching empirical observations. In contrast, Black-Scholes with constant volatility cannot generate clustering. Answer: High-volatility periods tend to be followed by high-volatility periods. Practical Use Case: Volatility clustering is critical for pricing and hedging path-dependent options like variance swaps and volatility swaps. Unlike Black-Scholes, Heston captures that after a high-volatility day, tomorrow is likely also high-volatility, affecting forward variance expectations. Risk managers use this property to model realistic VaR scenarios: clustered volatility means extreme events persist, requiring larger risk buffers. High-frequency trading strategies also exploit clustering by increasing activity during volatile periods when bid-ask spreads widen and profit opportunities expand.
