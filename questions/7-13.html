Definitions: A stochastic time change (or subordination) replaces calendar time $t$ with random time $\tau(t)$ in a process $X$. The time-changed process is $Y(t) = X(\tau(t))$, where $\tau(t)$ is an increasing stochastic process (chronometer or subordinator). Reasoning: If $\tau(t)$ is itself a Lévy process independent of $X$ (e.g., VG uses a Gamma subordinator), then $Y$ is still a Lévy process with independent increments. However, if $\tau(t)$ has dependent increments (e.g., $\tau(t) = \int_0^t v(s) ds$ where $v$ is a stochastic volatility process like Vasicek), then $Y(t)$ no longer has independent increments. This creates volatility clustering: periods of fast clock time ($\tau$ increasing rapidly) correspond to high volatility. Answer: A process that can exhibit volatility clustering. Practical Use Case: This concept is essential for modern financial engineering and quantitative trading. Practitioners apply these principles in derivatives pricing, risk management, and portfolio optimization across asset classes including equities, fixed income, commodities, and FX markets. Understanding this material enables quants to build robust pricing models, traders to identify arbitrage opportunities, and risk managers to compute accurate hedging strategies for complex derivative portfolios.
