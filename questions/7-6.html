Definitions: The Lévy measure $\Pi$ describes the intensity and size distribution of jumps in a Lévy process. The integral $\Pi(\mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\}) = \int_{\mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\}} \Pi(dx)$ represents the expected number of jumps per unit time (of any non-zero size). Reasoning: When $\Pi(\mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\}) = \infty$, the process experiences infinitely many jumps in any interval $[0,t]$ with $t > 0$. However, these are predominantly very small jumps (since $\int \min(1, x^2) \Pi(dx) < \infty$ still holds). Examples include the Gamma process and Variance Gamma process. In contrast, compound Poisson processes have $\Pi(\mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\}) < \infty$ (finite activity). Answer: Infinitely many jumps in any time interval of positive length. Practical Use Case: Infinite activity Levy processes like variance gamma and NIG (normal inverse Gaussian) are used to model high-frequency price dynamics where markets exhibit many small price changes interspersed with occasional large moves. Unlike finite-activity models (Merton jump-diffusion), infinite activity processes generate realistic tick-by-tick behavior without requiring discrete jump thresholds. When pricing short-dated options or modeling intraday risk, infinite activity captures the continuous stream of micro-jumps from order flow, improving hedging accuracy for high-gamma positions.
