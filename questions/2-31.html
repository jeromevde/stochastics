E[B(t$)^2$] for standard Brownian motion equals:
Definitions: Standard Brownian motion $B(t)$ starting at 0 has the property that $B(t) \sim N(0, t)$, meaning it's normally distributed with mean 0 and variance $t$.

Reasoning: For a random variable $X \sim N(0, \sigma^2)$ with mean 0, we have $E[X^2] = \text{Var}(X) + (E[X])^2 = \sigma^2 + 0 = \sigma^2$. Applying this to $B(t)$ with $\sigma^2 = t$, we get $E[B(t)^2] = t$. This also equals the quadratic variation $[B]_t$.

Answer: $E[B(t)^2] = t$.
