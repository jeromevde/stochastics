The value of the floating leg of a standard swap (at time t < T$\alpha$) equals:
Definitions: An interest rate swap has two legs: the floating leg pays LIBOR rates at each period, and the fixed leg pays a constant rate. The present value of the floating leg can be computed without knowing future LIBOR rates. Reasoning: Consider a strategy: at time $t$, invest $P(t,T_\alpha)$ dollars in a $T_\alpha$-bond. At $T_\alpha$, you receive 1 dollar. At each reset date, reinvest the principal plus LIBOR interest in the next-period bond. At $T_\beta$, you receive 1 dollar back. This replicating strategy shows that the floating leg is equivalent to buying a $T_\alpha$-bond and selling a $T_\beta$-bond, giving value $P(t,T_\alpha) - P(t,T_\beta)$. Answer: The floating leg value equals $P(t,T_\alpha) - P(t,T_\beta)$. Practical Use Case: This concept is essential for modern financial engineering and quantitative trading. Practitioners apply these principles in derivatives pricing, risk management, and portfolio optimization across asset classes including equities, fixed income, commodities, and FX markets. Understanding this material enables quants to build robust pricing models, traders to identify arbitrage opportunities, and risk managers to compute accurate hedging strategies for complex derivative portfolios.
