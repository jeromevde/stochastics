In the same 1-period model, a digital call paying 1 if S(1)=2 has time-0 value:
Definitions: A digital (binary) call option pays 1 if a condition is met (here, $S(1) = 2$) and 0 otherwise. With zero interest rate, the time-0 value equals the risk-neutral expected payoff. Reasoning: From the previous question, the risk-neutral probability of the up move is $q = \frac{1}{3}$. The digital call pays 1 in the up state and 0 in the down state. Thus: $$V(0) = E^\mathbb{Q}[V(1)] = \frac{1}{3} \cdot 1 + \frac{2}{3} \cdot 0 = \frac{1}{3}$$ Answer: The time-0 value is $\frac{1}{3}$. Practical Use Case: Digital options are used to construct complex payoffs and are actively traded. Traders use them to express directional views with capped payoffs. For example, a trader betting on a stock above $100 at expiry can buy a digital call instead of a vanilla call, paying less premium for bounded upside. Digital options are also building blocks: a call spread $(K_1, K_2)$ approximates a digital as $K_2 - K_1 \to 0$. Market makers hedge digitals by dynamically replicating them with call spreads. In FX markets, binary options are popular retail products. Investment banks also use digitals internally to decompose complex exotic payoffs into simpler components for risk management.
