Definitions: The risk-neutral world (or risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$) is a probability measure under which all assets, when discounted at the risk-free rate, become martingales. A martingale is a process whose expected future value equals its current value. Reasoning: Under $\mathbb{Q}$, the discounted stock price $e^{-rt}S_t$ must be a martingale. This means: $$\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[e^{-rt}S_t] = S_0$$ which implies: $$\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[S_t] = S_0 e^{rt}$$ Therefore, the expected growth rate of $S_t$ is exactly the risk-free rate $r$, not the actual historical return. Answer: In the risk-neutral world, stock prices grow at the risk-free rate to ensure no-arbitrage. Practical Use Case: This principle is fundamental to the Black-Scholes model and Monte Carlo simulation for derivatives pricing. When a quant prices a complex structured product (like an autocallable note), they simulate stock paths where the drift is the risk-free rate (currently around 4-5% for USD), not the stock's expected return of perhaps 10%. This ensures the simulated prices are arbitrage-free and consistent with liquid market prices of European calls and puts, which are used to calibrate the model's volatility parameters.
