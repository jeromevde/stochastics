Definitions: The tower law (or law of iterated expectations) describes how conditional expectations behave when $\sigma$-algebras are nested: $\mathcal{G}_1 \subseteq \mathcal{G}_2 \subseteq \mathcal{F}$. Here, $\mathcal{G}_1$ represents coarser (less) information than $\mathcal{G}_2$. Reasoning: When we condition first on $\mathcal{G}_2$ (more information) and then on $\mathcal{G}_1$ (less information), we "forget" the extra information from $\mathcal{G}_2$, leaving only the $\mathcal{G}_1$ information. This gives the same result as conditioning directly on $\mathcal{G}_1$. Think of it as: if you first learn details then forget them, you're left with the coarse information. Answer: The tower law states $E[E[X|\mathcal{G}_2]|\mathcal{G}_1] = E[X|\mathcal{G}_1]$ almost surely.
