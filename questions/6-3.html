In the Heston model, the correlation $\rho$ between asset and variance Brownian motions is typically set to $\rho$ < 0 for equities because:
Definitions: In the Heston model, the correlation parameter $\rho$ measures the instantaneous correlation between the Brownian motions driving the stock price and the variance: $dW_S \cdot dW_v = \rho \, dt$.

Reasoning: Setting $\rho < 0$ (negative correlation) captures the "leverage effect": when stock prices fall, volatility tends to increase, and vice versa. This creates asymmetry in the return distribution - negative shocks come with increased uncertainty. The result is a volatility skew where OTM puts (low strikes) have higher implied volatility than OTM calls, matching the observed equity market pattern. Positive $\rho$ would create the opposite effect.

Answer: $\rho < 0$ produces a volatility skew (lower strikes have higher implied vol).
