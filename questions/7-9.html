Infinite divisibility of a distribution means:
Definitions: A probability distribution is infinitely divisible if, for every positive integer $n$, it can be represented as the distribution of the sum of $n$ independent and identically distributed random variables. This is a key property connecting distributions to Lévy processes. Reasoning: Formally, $X$ has an infinitely divisible distribution if for every $n \geq 1$, there exist i.i.d. random variables $Y_1, \ldots, Y_n$ such that $X \stackrel{d}{=} Y_1 + \cdots + Y_n$. The connection to Lévy processes: if $X(t)$ is a Lévy process, then $X(1)$ has an infinitely divisible distribution (divide $[0,1]$ into $n$ equal parts and use independent increments). Conversely, every infinitely divisible distribution is the law of $X(1)$ for some Lévy process. Answer: For any $n$, it can be written as the sum of $n$ i.i.d. random variables. Practical Use Case: Infinite divisibility is the mathematical foundation for modeling asset returns over multiple time horizons consistently. If daily log-returns follow an infinitely divisible distribution (e.g., variance gamma), then weekly and monthly returns automatically have well-defined distributions from the same family via aggregation. This ensures model consistency when pricing options at different maturities and underlies the link between Levy processes and option pricing: the characteristic function of an infinitely divisible distribution determines the entire term structure of option prices via Fourier methods.
