The Borel $\sigma$-algebra on $\mathbb{R}$ is generated by:
Definitions: The Borel $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$ is the smallest $\sigma$-algebra containing all open sets in $\mathbb{R}$. A generating set for a $\sigma$-algebra is a collection of sets such that the $\sigma$-algebra is the smallest one containing that collection. Reasoning: $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$ is defined as $\sigma(\mathcal{O})$, where $\mathcal{O}$ is the collection of all open sets. By the properties of $\sigma$-algebras, closed sets, open intervals $(a,b)$, closed intervals $[a,b]$, and many other sets are all in $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$. However, the power set of $\mathbb{R}$ is much larger and contains non-Borel sets. Answer: The Borel $\sigma$-algebra is generated by all open sets (or equivalently, all open intervals).
