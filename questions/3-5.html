Consider a 1-period model: S(0)=1, S(1)=2 (prob p) or S(1)=$\tfrac{1}{2}$ (prob 1âˆ’p), zero interest rate. The risk-neutral upward probability is:
Definitions: In a risk-neutral measure, the expected return on all assets equals the risk-free rate. With zero interest rate, this means the current stock price equals the risk-neutral expected future price. Let $q$ be the risk-neutral probability of the up move. Reasoning: The risk-neutral pricing condition with $r=0$ gives: $S(0) = E^\mathbb{Q}[S(1)] = q \cdot 2 + (1-q) \cdot \frac{1}{2}$. Substituting $S(0) = 1$: $1 = 2q + \frac{1}{2} - \frac{q}{2} = \frac{3q}{2} + \frac{1}{2}$. Solving: $\frac{1}{2} = \frac{3q}{2}$, so $q = \frac{1}{3}$. Answer: The risk-neutral upward probability is $\frac{1}{3}$.
