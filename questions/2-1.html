Definitions: A $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{F}$ on a set $\Omega$ is a collection of subsets that satisfies: $$\begin{align} &\text{(1) } \Omega \in \mathcal{F} \\ &\text{(2) If } A \in \mathcal{F} \text{ then } A^c \in \mathcal{F} \text{ (closed under complements)} \\ &\text{(3) If } A_1, A_2, \ldots \in \mathcal{F} \text{ then } \bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i \in \mathcal{F} \text{ (closed under countable unions)} \end{align}$$ Reasoning: The definition only requires closure under countable unions. While this implies closure under finite unions (as a special case), it does not extend to arbitrary uncountable unions. In fact, most $\sigma$-algebras are not closed under uncountable unions. Answer: Closure under arbitrary uncountable unions is not required, making option D the exception. Practical Use Case: In quantitative finance, $\sigma$-algebras represent information available to traders at different times. For example, the filtration $(\mathcal{F}_t)_{t \ge 0}$ represents the market information up to time $t$. The countability restriction ensures that we can compute probabilities and expectations using measure theory. When building trading strategies, we ensure that trading decisions at time $t$ depend only on information in $\mathcal{F}_t$, making strategies "adapted" to the filtration. This prevents look-ahead bias in backtesting and ensures strategies are implementable in real markets.
