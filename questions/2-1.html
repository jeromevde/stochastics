A $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{F}$ on a set $\Omega$ must satisfy all of the following EXCEPT:
Definitions: A $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{F}$ on a set $\Omega$ is a collection of subsets that satisfies: (1) $\Omega \in \mathcal{F}$, (2) if $A \in \mathcal{F}$ then $A^c \in \mathcal{F}$ (closed under complements), and (3) if $A_1, A_2, \ldots \in \mathcal{F}$ then $\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i \in \mathcal{F}$ (closed under countable unions). Reasoning: The definition only requires closure under countable unions. While this implies closure under finite unions (as a special case), it does not extend to arbitrary uncountable unions. In fact, most $\sigma$-algebras are not closed under uncountable unions. Answer: Closure under arbitrary uncountable unions is not required, making option D the exception.
