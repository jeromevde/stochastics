In the Black-Scholes model, if $\sigma$ = 0 and r is constant, what is the call price C for $S_0$ = 100, K = 90, r = 5%, T = 1? (Round to 2 decimal places)
Definitions: In the Black-Scholes model with zero volatility ($\sigma = 0$), the stock price evolution becomes deterministic (no randomness). The stock grows at the risk-free rate: $$S(T) = S_0 e^{rT}$$ The call option payoff is $(S(T) - K)^+ = \max(S(T) - K, 0)$, discounted to present value. Reasoning: Step-by-step calculation with given parameters $S_0 = 100$, $K = 90$, $r = 0.05$, $T = 1$, $\sigma = 0$: 1. Future stock price: $S(1) = 100 \cdot e^{0.05 \cdot 1} = 100 \cdot e^{0.05} \approx 105.127$ 2. Payoff at maturity: $\max(105.127 - 90, 0) = 15.127$ 3. Present value (discount at $r$): $C = e^{-0.05} \cdot 15.127 = 0.95123 \cdot 15.127 \approx 14.39$ With zero volatility, there's no uncertainty—the option price is simply the discounted deterministic payoff. Alternatively, note that $C = (S_0 - Ke^{-rT})^+ = (100 - 90e^{-0.05})^+ = (100 - 85.61)^+ = 14.39$. Answer: The call price is approximately 14.39. Practical Use Case: The zero-volatility limit serves as a sanity check for option pricing implementations and understanding intrinsic value. When $\sigma = 0$, the call price reduces to $\max(S_0 - Ke^{-rT}, 0)$—the forward intrinsic value. Traders use this as a lower bound: actual options with $\sigma > 0$ must be worth at least this value due to time value. For testing option pricing libraries, quants verify that setting $\sigma \to 0$ produces this formula. In deep in-the-money options where vega is negligible, the price approximates the zero-vol limit. For corporate finance, when valuing employee stock options with restricted selling, reduced volatility scenarios approach this limit. Understanding this limit also helps explain put-call parity: $C - P = S_0 - Ke^{-rT}$ exactly when $\sigma = 0$, with time value disappearing.
