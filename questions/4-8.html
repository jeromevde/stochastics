In the Vasicek model, r(t)|r(s) has distribution:
Definitions: The conditional distribution of $r(t)$ given $r(s)$ (where $s < t$) describes how the short rate evolves over time in the Vasicek model. Since the SDE is linear in $r$ with additive Gaussian noise, the solution remains Gaussian (normal). Reasoning: The Vasicek SDE $dr = \kappa(\theta - r) \, dt + \sigma \, dB$ can be solved explicitly using an integrating factor. The solution is: $$r(t) = \theta + (r(s) - \theta)e^{-\kappa(t-s)} + \sigma \int_s^t e^{-\kappa(t-u)} \, dB(u)$$ Since the stochastic integral is normal, the conditional distribution is: $$r(t) \mid r(s) \sim N\left(\theta + (r(s) - \theta)e^{-\kappa(t-s)}, \frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa}(1 - e^{-2\kappa(t-s)})\right)$$ The mean shows exponential decay toward $\theta$ at rate $\kappa$. The variance increases from 0 (when $t=s$) to the stationary variance $\sigma^2/(2\kappa)$ as $t \to \infty$. Being Gaussian allows tractable bond pricing formulas but means rates can go negative. Answer: The conditional distribution is Normal. Practical Use Case: The Gaussian conditional distribution enables closed-form bond pricing and efficient Monte Carlo simulation. Traders simulate interest rate paths exactly (without discretization error) by sampling $Z \sim N(0,1)$ and computing $r(t) = \theta + (r(s)-\theta)e^{-\kappa(t-s)} + \sqrt{\frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa}(1-e^{-2\kappa(t-s)})} Z$. For bond options (caps, floors, swaptions), the normality allows analytical pricing formulas. Quants also use the conditional distribution for scenario analysis: given current rate $r(0)$, they compute confidence intervals for future rates using the mean and variance formulas. The explicit formulas make Vasicek popular for risk management and asset-liability management where thousands of rate scenarios must be generated quickly.
