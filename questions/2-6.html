The $\sigma$-algebra generated by a random variable X is:
Definitions: The $\sigma$-algebra generated by a random variable $X: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ is the smallest $\sigma$-algebra on $\Omega$ that makes $X$ measurable. $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$ denotes the Borel $\sigma$-algebra on $\mathbb{R}$, and $X^{-1}(U) = \{\omega \in \Omega : X(\omega) \in U\}$ is the preimage of $U$ under $X$.

Reasoning: By definition, $\sigma(X) = \{X^{-1}(U) : U \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\}$ consists of all events whose occurrence can be determined by knowing the value of $X$. This captures exactly the information that $X$ provides: we can answer questions like "Is $X \in U$?" for any Borel set $U$, but no more.

Answer: The correct answer is $\{X^{-1}(U) : U \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\}$, which is the collection of all preimages of Borel sets.
