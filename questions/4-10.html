The Vasicek zero-coupon bond price P(t,T) = A(t,T)$\cdot$e^{−B(t,T)$\cdot$r(t)} has an 'affine' structure because:
Definitions: An affine term structure model is one where bond prices have the exponential-affine form: $$P(t,T) = A(t,T) e^{-B(t,T) r(t)}$$ equivalently, taking logarithms: $$\ln P(t,T) = \ln A(t,T) - B(t,T) r(t)$$ "Affine" means "linear plus constant"—a function of the form $f(x) = a + bx$. Reasoning: The log bond price is $$\ln P(t,T) = \ln A(t,T) - B(t,T) \cdot r(t) = \text{constant} + \text{coefficient} \times r(t)$$ which is an affine (linear) function of the short rate $r(t)$. This structure is extremely powerful because: 1. Bond prices are explicit functions of the current short rate $r(t)$ 2. The entire yield curve is determined by $r(t)$ and time-dependent functions $A(t,T)$ and $B(t,T)$ 3. The functions $A$ and $B$ satisfy Riccati ODEs that can be solved analytically in Vasicek 4. Option pricing uses expectations involving exponentials of affine functions, which remain tractable The affine class includes Vasicek, CIR, Hull-White, and many multi-factor extensions. Answer: It's affine because $\ln P$ is an affine (linear) function of $r$. Practical Use Case: Affine models dominate interest rate derivatives pricing due to computational efficiency. For vanilla bond options and interest rate caps/floors, traders use closed-form pricing formulas that arise from the affine structure—no Monte Carlo needed. The affine property also enables fast calibration: given market bond prices $P^{\text{market}}(t,T_i)$, quants extract the current short rate $r(t)$ by inverting the affine formula. For large portfolios with thousands of positions, affine models allow real-time risk calculations (computing Greeks) that would be infeasible with non-affine models requiring numerical PDE or Monte Carlo. Investment banks' fixed income trading systems rely heavily on affine models: Vasicek and Hull-White for EUR/USD swaps, CIR for credit spreads, multi-factor affine models for mortgages. The ability to write $\ln P = a - br$ means all interest rate sensitivities (duration, convexity) follow from simple derivatives of affine functions.
