According to the fundamental asset pricing theorem, how do we compute the fair value of a derivative?
Definitions: The fundamental asset pricing theorem states that in a no-arbitrage market, any derivative's fair value equals the expected payoff discounted at the risk-free rate, where the expectation is taken under the risk-neutral (equivalent martingale) measure. Reasoning: Under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$, all assets grow at the risk-free rate $r$ on average. This allows us to compute the fair value as $V_0 = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[e^{-rT} \text{Payoff}]$. The theorem guarantees this price prevents arbitrage opportunities. Answer: We compute the discounted expected payoff in the risk-neutral world, not the real world, because the risk-neutral measure ensures no-arbitrage pricing.
