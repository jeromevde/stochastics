A filtration ($\mathcal{F}_t$) on a probability space must satisfy:
Definitions: A filtration $(\mathcal{F}_t)_{t \geq 0}$ is a family of $\sigma$-algebras indexed by time, where each $\mathcal{F}_t \subseteq \mathcal{F}$ represents the information available up to time $t$. The key property is: $$t \le s \implies \mathcal{F}_t \subseteq \mathcal{F}_s$$ This means information accumulates over timeâ€”once something is known, it remains known. Reasoning: For times $t \leq s$, we must have $\mathcal{F}_t \subseteq \mathcal{F}_s$ because information at time $s$ includes all the information that was available at the earlier time $t$. This increasing property models the natural flow of information: you can't forget what you've learned, only gain more knowledge. Answer: A filtration satisfies $\mathcal{F}_t \subseteq \mathcal{F}_s$ for all $t \leq s$ (increasing in time). Practical Use Case: In derivatives pricing, the natural filtration $\mathcal{F}_t = \sigma(S_u : 0 \le u \le t)$ captures the market information from observing stock prices up to time $t$. As time progresses, traders observe more price data, so $\mathcal{F}_t$ grows. This framework allows us to price path-dependent derivatives correctly. For example, an Asian option's payoff depends on $\text{Avg}(S_0, \ldots, S_T)$. At time $t$, we know the average-so-far from $\mathcal{F}_t$, and we condition the remaining expectation on this information. The increasing property ensures that all previously observed prices remain in our information set when computing conditional expectations.
