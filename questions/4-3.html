In the Black-Scholes model under the risk-neutral measure, the drift $\mu$(t) must equal:
Definitions: In the Black-Scholes model, stock prices follow GBM under both the real-world and risk-neutral measures, but with different drift parameters. Under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$, the discounted stock price must be a martingale.

Reasoning: The risk-neutral condition requires $E^\mathbb{Q}[S(t)/B(t)] = S(0)/B(0)$, where $B(t) = e^{rt}$ is the bank account. For $S(t)/B(t)$ to be a martingale, the drift of $S$ under $\mathbb{Q}$ must equal the growth rate of the numeraire, which is $r$. This means we must have $\mu = r$ in the risk-neutral SDE $dS = rS \, dt + \sigma S \, dB^\mathbb{Q}$.

Answer: Under the risk-neutral measure, the drift must equal $\mu(t) = r(t)$, the risk-free rate.
