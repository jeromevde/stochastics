Definitions: In the Vasicek model, bond pricing requires computing the distribution of the integrated short rate $I_T = \int_0^T r(u) \, du$. Since $r$ is Gaussian, this integral is also Gaussian, characterized by its mean and variance conditional on $r(0)$. Reasoning: The variance formula for the integrated rate is: $$\text{Var}\left[\int_0^T r(u) \, du \Big| r(0)\right] = \frac{\sigma^2}{\kappa^2}\left(T - B(0,T) - \frac{\kappa B(0,T)^2}{2}\right)$$ where $B(0,T) = \frac{1}{\kappa}(1 - e^{-\kappa T})$ is the bond duration function depending on $\kappa$ and $T$. Key observations: - Depends on volatility $\sigma$ (obviously—more volatility increases variance) - Depends on mean-reversion speed $\kappa$ (affects how quickly rates decorrelate) - Depends on maturity $T$ (longer maturities accumulate more variance) - Does NOT depend on long-term mean $\theta$ (affects mean but not variance) - Does NOT depend on initial rate $r(0)$ (variance is independent of starting point) Answer: The variance depends on $\sigma$, $\kappa$, and $T$. Practical Use Case: Understanding variance of integrated rates is critical for bond option pricing and yield curve uncertainty. The formula shows that mean reversion ($\kappa$) reduces long-term variance compared to random walks: as $\kappa \to 0$ (no reversion), variance grows like $T^3$, but with reversion, growth is slower. Traders use this when pricing bond options (caps, floors): the variance determines option implied volatility. For risk management, portfolio managers assess "convexity" (sensitivity to yield volatility) using this variance formula. When calibrating Vasicek to swaption volatilities, quants fit $\kappa$ and $\sigma$ jointly to match market-implied variances across maturities. The independence from $\theta$ and $r(0)$ simplifies calibration: these parameters only affect mean levels, not uncertainty measures.
