The swap rate S_{$\alpha,\beta$}(t) equals:
Definitions: The swap rate $S_{\alpha,\beta}(t)$ is the fixed rate in an interest rate swap that makes the swap have zero value at initiation. A swap exchanges fixed payments at rate $S$ for floating LIBOR payments over periods from $T_\alpha$ to $T_\beta$. Reasoning: The floating leg has present value $P(t,T_\alpha) - P(t,T_\beta)$ (this is a standard result from bond pricing). The fixed leg has present value $S \sum_{j=\alpha+1}^{\beta} \tau_j P(t,T_j)$, where $\tau_j$ are the accrual periods. Setting these equal and solving for $S$ gives: $S_{\alpha,\beta}(t) = \frac{P(t,T_\alpha) - P(t,T_\beta)}{\sum_{j=\alpha+1}^{\beta} \tau_j P(t,T_j)}$. Answer: $S_{\alpha,\beta}(t) = \frac{P(t,T_\alpha) - P(t,T_\beta)}{\sum_j P(t,T_j)\tau_j}$. Practical Use Case: Swap rates are the foundation for interest rate swap trading and benchmarking. Market makers continuously compute $S_{\alpha,\beta}(t)$ from the observed term structure of zero-coupon bond prices $P(t,T_j)$ to quote bid-offer spreads to clients. This formula also enables arbitrage detection: if a trader observes quoted swap rates inconsistent with bond prices, they can execute a cash-and-carry strategy buying/selling the bond portfolio and entering the opposite swap position to lock in risk-free profit.
