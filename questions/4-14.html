Definitions: The CIR model has an exact solution for the transition density $p(r(T) \mid r(t))$. Due to the square-root volatility and mean reversion, the distribution is related to the chi-squared family but with a non-zero noncentrality parameter (noncentral chi-squared). Reasoning: The conditional distribution can be expressed as: $$c \cdot r(T) \mid r(t) \sim \chi^2(d, \lambda)$$ a noncentral chi-squared distribution with degrees of freedom $d = 4\kappa\theta/\sigma^2$ and noncentrality parameter $\lambda$ depending on $r(t)$. More precisely: $$\frac{4\kappa}{\sigma^2(1-e^{-\kappa(T-t)})} r(T) \sim \chi^2\left(\frac{4\kappa\theta}{\sigma^2}, \frac{4\kappa e^{-\kappa(T-t)}}{\sigma^2(1-e^{-\kappa(T-t)})} r(t)\right)$$ This distribution is positively skewed (asymmetric), reflecting the non-negativity constraint and the dynamics of interest rates. Unlike Vasicek's normal distribution, the noncentral chi-squared naturally lives on $(0, \infty)$. Answer: The CIR short rate follows a noncentral chi-squared distribution. Practical Use Case: The explicit noncentral chi-squared distribution enables analytical bond option pricing and efficient simulation in CIR models. Traders price caps and floors using closed-form formulas involving the noncentral chi-squared CDF, which are faster than Monte Carlo. For path-dependent derivatives like Asian options on interest rates, Monte Carlo simulations sample from this distribution exactly at each time step, avoiding discretization bias. The distribution's skewness captures realistic features: upward rate spikes are more likely than downward spikes. Quants use the noncentral chi-squared density for maximum likelihood estimation when calibrating CIR to historical rate data. Credit risk models use CIR for default intensities, and the noncentral chi-squared distribution provides exact survival probability calculations. Statistical libraries implement efficient noncentral chi-squared samplers specifically for CIR simulation in financial applications.
