If B(t) is standard Brownian motion, what is E[$\int_0^t$ B(s) dB(s)]?
Definitions: The Itô integral $\int_0^t B(s) \, dB(s)$ is a stochastic integral of an adapted process against Brownian motion. A key property of Itô integrals is that they are martingales (when the integrand satisfies integrability conditions). Reasoning: Since the Itô integral is a martingale starting at 0, its expectation at any time is: $$E\left[\int_0^t B(s) \, dB(s)\right] = 0$$ Alternatively, we know from earlier that $\int_0^t B(s) \, dB(s) = \frac{1}{2}(B(t)^2 - t)$, so: $$E\left[\int_0^t B(s) \, dB(s)\right] = \frac{1}{2}(E[B(t)^2] - t) = \frac{1}{2}(t - t) = 0$$ Answer: The expectation is 0. Practical Use Case: The zero-expectation property of Itô integrals guarantees that dynamic trading strategies with no initial cost have zero expected profit under the risk-neutral measure, preventing arbitrage. When a trader implements a delta-hedging strategy for an option, the cumulative profit/loss from hedging is $\int_0^T \Delta_t dS_t$, an Itô integral. Under the risk-neutral measure, this has zero expectation, consistent with the option being fairly priced. In practice, P&L from hedging deviates from zero due to gamma exposure, transaction costs, and discrete rebalancing—but the theoretical zero expectation provides the benchmark for evaluating hedge performance.
