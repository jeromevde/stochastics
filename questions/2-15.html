The quadratic variation [B]$_t$ of a standard Brownian motion B(t) equals:
Definitions: The quadratic variation $[B]_t$ of a process $B(t)$ is the limit of sums of squared increments: $\lim_{\|\Pi\| \to 0} \sum_{t_i \in \Pi} (B(t_{i+1}) - B(t_i))^2$, where $\Pi$ is a partition of $[0,t]$. For smooth functions, this would be zero, but Brownian motion is rougher.

Reasoning: For standard Brownian motion, each increment $B(t_{i+1}) - B(t_i)$ has variance $t_{i+1} - t_i$. When we sum the squared increments, the contributions add up to $t$ in the limit. This is captured by the formal notation $(dB)^2 = dt$. The quadratic variation being non-zero reflects the fractal nature of Brownian paths.

Answer: The quadratic variation is $[B]_t = t$.
