Definitions: A local martingale is a process $X$ for which there exists an increasing sequence of stopping times $\tau_k \to \infty$ almost surely such that each stopped process: $$X^{\tau_k}_t = X_{\min(\tau_k, t)}$$ is a true martingale. This weakens the integrability requirement of martingales. Reasoning: Local martingales satisfy the martingale property "locally" (up to stopping times), even if they may blow up or fail to be integrable globally. The stopped versions $X^{\tau_k}$ are true martingales, meaning $E[X^{\tau_k}_t | \mathcal{F}_s] = X^{\tau_k}_s$ for $s < t$. This class includes stochastic integrals and is central to stochastic calculus. Answer: The stopped versions are martingales. Practical Use Case: Local martingales arise naturally in incomplete markets and when modeling assets with stochastic volatility. For example, in the Heston model, even though the discounted stock price is not always a martingale due to technicalities, it is a local martingale, which suffices for no-arbitrage pricing under appropriate conditions. Traders must be careful: local martingales can exhibit "bubbles" where $E[S_T] < S_0$ despite the martingale-like property, leading to pricing anomalies. This is why practitioners verify that their asset price models are true martingales (not just local martingales) under the risk-neutral measure to avoid arbitrage issues.
