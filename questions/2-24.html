A continuously differentiable function has quadratic variation equal to:
Definitions: The quadratic variation of a function $f$ on $[0,t]$ is $\lim_{\|\Pi\| \to 0} \sum_{i} (f(t_i) - f(t_{i-1}))^2$, where $\Pi$ is a partition. It measures the "roughness" of the function at a fine scale.

Reasoning: For a continuously differentiable function, each increment satisfies $f(t_i) - f(t_{i-1}) \approx f'(t_{i-1})(t_i - t_{i-1}) = O(\Delta t)$. Therefore, $(f(t_i) - f(t_{i-1}))^2 = O((\Delta t)^2)$. Summing over $n$ intervals where $\Delta t \approx t/n$, we get $\sum O((\Delta t)^2) = O(n \cdot (t/n)^2) = O(t/n) \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$.

Answer: A continuously differentiable function has quadratic variation equal to 0.
