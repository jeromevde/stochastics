The halvation time $\tau$ in the Vasicek model satisfies e^{−$\kappa\tau$} = $\tfrac{1}{2}$. It represents:
Definitions: The half-life (or halvation time) $\tau$ is the time it takes for a deviation from the long-term mean $\theta$ to reduce to half its initial value on average. It characterizes the speed of mean reversion and is defined by $e^{-\kappa\tau} = \frac{1}{2}$. Reasoning: From the Vasicek solution, the conditional expectation is $$\mathbb{E}[r(t) \mid r(s)] = \theta + (r(s) - \theta)e^{-\kappa(t-s)}$$ The deviation from $\theta$ at time $t$ is $(r(s) - \theta)e^{-\kappa(t-s)}$. For this deviation to equal half the initial deviation $(r(s) - \theta)/2$, we need: $$(r(s) - \theta)e^{-\kappa \tau} = \frac{1}{2}(r(s) - \theta)$$ Canceling $(r(s) - \theta)$ and solving: $e^{-\kappa \tau} = \frac{1}{2}$, which gives $$\tau = \frac{\ln(2)}{\kappa} \approx \frac{0.693}{\kappa}$$ Larger $\kappa$ means faster mean reversion and shorter half-life. For example, if $\kappa = 0.2$, then $\tau \approx 3.5$ years. Answer: It represents the time for the deviation from $\theta$ to halve (on average). Practical Use Case: The half-life $\tau = \ln(2)/\kappa$ is crucial for calibrating interest rate models and assessing reversion speeds. When fitting Vasicek to historical data, quants estimate $\kappa$ from rate time series, then compute $\tau$ to verify realism. Typical values: short rates like Fed Funds have $\kappa \approx 0.3$ (half-life ~2-3 years), while long-term rates have slower reversion ($\kappa \approx 0.1$, half-life ~7 years). Portfolio managers use half-life to time bond trades: if rates are far from $\theta$, expect reversion within $\tau$ to $2\tau$. For liability hedging, pension funds match asset durations to liability durations by considering mean reversion—longer half-lives reduce duration compared to random walk models. Traders also use $\tau$ for relative value: if two correlated rates have different half-lives, the faster-reverting one offers better mean-reversion trading opportunities.
